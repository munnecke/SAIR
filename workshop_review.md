# 2026 Science and AI Summit at UC Riverside: A Comprehensive Review

## Overview

The 2026 Science and AI Summit, held at UC Riverside, brought together Nobel laureates, Fields Medalists, experimental physicists, computer scientists, mathematicians, and industry figures for a full day of talks and panels organized around a single animating conviction: that science and artificial intelligence must be developed together, not in parallel. The event was co-organized by the SIR (Science and AI Research) Foundation, whose three co-founders — Barry Barish, Nobel laureate and gravitational wave pioneer; Terence Tao, Fields Medalist and UCLA mathematician; and Chuck Ing, entrepreneur and Foundation co-founder — set the intellectual agenda. UCR served as institutional host, lending the summit a regional dimension: the Inland Empire and Southern California were framed not merely as backdrop but as an underappreciated concentration of scientific and technological talent whose moment, organizers argued, had arrived.

The day's program unfolded across three broad intellectual tracks that Ing articulated in his opening remarks: how AI is changing the design and operation of scientific instruments; how AI enables simulation and analysis of scientific data at scales and speeds previously unimaginable; and how AI might help build theory and help science think differently. These tracks proved genuinely distinct in character. The instrumentation discussions were grounded in engineering constraints — bandwidth, power budgets, FPGA latency — and debated practical co-design strategies. The simulation and analysis panels were more methodologically diverse, ranging from cosmological emulators to tensor decomposition to neural operators. The theory-building conversations were the most speculative and also the most philosophically charged, touching on formal verification, sparse-reward reinforcement learning, and the nature of mathematical knowledge itself. Woven through all three tracks was a set of tensions that the summit surfaced but did not resolve: between AI as accelerator and AI as discoverer; between open access and national security; between the excitement of AI-generated hypotheses and the unsolved problem of validating them rigorously.

UCR's institutional identity shaped the event in ways that went beyond hospitality. The university's unusual profile — simultaneously an R1 research institution, a Hispanic Serving Institution, an AAPI Serving Institution, and the top-ranked university for social mobility in the United States for five of the last seven years — gave the summit an equity dimension that distinguished it from comparable events at more elite-coastal venues. Speakers returned repeatedly to the question of who gets access to frontier AI tools, and the gap between that aspiration and current reality was among the day's sharpest unresolved tensions.

## Opening Addresses

### [UCR Host Welcome / Chuck Ing / Barry Barish Opening](presentations/ucr-welcome-and-sir-foundation-opening.md) · [[ucr-welcome-and-sir-foundation-opening]] · [▶ 0:16:36](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=996)

The summit opened with a UCR administrator framing the university's research identity around a metaphor that would recur across the day: curiosity-driven fundamental science as a "master key" capable of opening many doors, contrasted with applied research, which opens only specific ones. The framing was not merely rhetorical — it served to position UCR's cross-disciplinary RACE Institute (Responsible AI Research and Education), which unites over 85 faculty across colleges, as an institutional embodiment of this principle. The Southern California Oasis initiative and a forthcoming innovation hub were presented as the commercialization layer bridging university research to regional economic development, with sustainable transportation and precision agriculture as anchor sectors.

### Chuck Ing — SIR Foundation Mission

Chuck Ing's opening remarks were brief but precise in their framing. The SIR Foundation's founding conviction, he explained, is that science and AI need to be "in the same room" — not merely applied to each other at arm's length, but developed through genuine co-creation. Southern California, including the Inland Empire, he argued, represents an underappreciated concentration of scientific talent: the talent is there, but the infrastructure to coordinate and amplify it has been missing. The SIR Foundation's ambition is to supply that infrastructure.

### Barry Barish — The Instrumentation Gap

Barish spoke from his experience both at LIGO and at UCR's Center for Experimental Cosmology and Instrumentation, which he helped build after joining the faculty in 2018. His core argument identified a structural gap in how research universities hire: institutions tend to recruit physicists skilled at analyzing data and publishing results, while systematically neglecting the experimentalists and instrumentation experts whose work makes the data possible in the first place. Dark matter searches, cosmic microwave background studies, and next-generation gravitational wave detectors all depend on technical expertise that is undervalued and underrepresented in standard faculty hiring. He framed the SIR Foundation as the vehicle for merging this kind of deep experimental physics work with AI capabilities — not as a theoretical exercise but as a practical necessity for the next generation of instruments.

### [Terence Tao — Infrastructure for AI-Augmented Mathematics](presentations/terence-tao-ai-augmented-mathematics.md) · [[terence-tao-ai-augmented-mathematics]] · [▶ 0:33:13](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=1993)

Tao's opening address was the most intellectually ambitious of the day and provided the summit's central organizing metaphor. Mathematics, he observed, has operated on essentially the same model for centuries: small groups of researchers, handcrafted proofs, slow peer review, chalk blackboards. This model does not scale — and AI is now stressing it past its limits. He coined the term "proof indigestion" to describe the current situation: AI systems are generating solutions to mathematical problems faster than existing infrastructure — journals, referees, conferences — can process. The result is a flood of AI-generated papers of variable quality that human referees cannot evaluate fast enough, alongside problem-solving platforms with backlogs of unverified submissions.

Tao's central analogy was the automobile. Early cars were forced onto narrow cobblestone streets designed for horses, producing chaos. The solution was not better cars but redesigned infrastructure: dedicated roads, pedestrian walkways, hybrid zones that served multiple modes of transit simultaneously. Mathematics and science, Tao argued, need analogous infrastructure redesign for AI — dedicated channels capable of absorbing AI-generated output without crowding out human contributions.

He then described two active SIR competitions as prototypes of this new infrastructure. The first, a "distillation challenge" launched on Pi Day, draws on a prior project that generated 22 million true/false algebra questions. Contestants submit a one-page "cheat sheet" that improves the performance of cheap, open-source AI models on these problems; leading submissions have lifted accuracy from roughly 51% to roughly 80%. The second, run in cooperation with the LMFDB (L-Functions and Modular Forms Database), is framed as an "Easter egg hunt" for the inverse Galois problem: there are 160,000 types (Galois groups) of degree-24 polynomials, and the challenge is to find one polynomial of each type. Common types are straightforward; rare types reveal empirical data about Galois group frequency that mathematics has traditionally been unable to study experimentally. Tao noted that this competition is generating genuine excitement at algebra conferences precisely because it opens a capability orthogonal to traditional mathematical work, rather than competing with it. He proposed scaling these competitions with larger prizes and extending the model to other sciences where verification can be automated.

### [SIR Platform Overview](presentations/sir-platform-overview.md) · [[sir-platform-overview]] · [▶ 0:55:54](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=3354)

A SIR team member followed with a presentation on the platform architecture underlying these competitions. The vision is an open science and AI collaboration platform organized around four verticals: science, industry, builders, and capital. Infrastructure elements described included a persistent research identity ("SID") that records verified contributions portably across competitions and events; "SIR Connect," a problem-based researcher matchmaking system distinct from generic professional profiles; a shared computational playground for running and comparing solvers; and open benchmarks for measuring progress across models and teams. The stated ambition spans mathematics, physics, biology, materials science, chemistry, energy, and robotics, with an explicit design principle of accessibility for researchers at under-resourced universities and in emerging economies. The presenter's emphasis on reusability of artifacts — not merely results — reflected a philosophy that scientific discovery compounds when intermediate work is made visible and usable by others.

## [Panel: AI-Driven Experimental Design](presentations/panel-ai-driven-experimental-design.md) · [[panel-ai-driven-experimental-design]] · [▶ 1:45:58](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=6358)

The morning's first panel brought together researchers working at the instrumentation frontier of particle physics, gravitational wave detection, accelerator design, and dark matter detection. The discussions were technically dense and collectively surfaced a theme that would echo throughout the day: the biggest gains from AI may come not from optimizing existing systems but from co-designing instruments and their control systems together from the outset.

### RA Adikari — Reinforcement Learning for LIGO Control

Adikari argued forcefully that viewing experiments as primarily hardware with incidental software is a "20th century view." Modern detectors like LIGO inject quantum states and require coherent quantum control to extract maximum performance. LIGO runs hundreds of coupled feedback control loops in real time that have never been fully understood as an integrated system — and the conventional approach of tuning them individually has reached its limits. Reinforcement learning's advantage, Adikari explained, is that it does not require understanding each loop in isolation; it only requires a good simulator. But the compute cost is severe: a 2024 demonstration of RL-based control for a single LIGO sub-system required 36 hours of computation. Scaling to the full detector is not currently feasible. The path forward, he argued, requires both better approximations and a fundamental rethinking of detector design — building the control system and the physical instrument together from inception rather than treating control as a retrofit problem. He also offered a more contrarian observation: rather than always scaling up to bigger instruments, there is "a lot of room at the bottom." LIGO's effective signal-to-noise relative to the theoretical spacetime noise floor is only about 10 out of a possible 10^11 — an "F-minus grade" driven by materials limitations, specifically two-level systems in thin films that cause 1/f noise in cryogenic, microwave, and optical detectors. Engineering detector films with the stability of nuclear clocks, he suggested, could be more transformative than building larger instruments — but requires quantum simulation capabilities that do not yet exist.

### Daniel Ratner — Bayesian Algorithm Execution and Foundation Models

Ratner introduced BAX (Bayesian Algorithm Execution) as a significant generalization of standard Bayesian optimization. Where conventional Bayesian optimization fits a Gaussian process surrogate to a single scalar objective, BAX allows the surrogate to learn underlying physics — decoupling the surrogate model from any fixed objective function and making the approach far more general. The method has already been demonstrated for designing and controlling multiple types of particle accelerators, and is being extended to materials science and drug design. Ratner's more speculative proposal was "Foundation BAX": using a large foundation model as the surrogate, so that the output of a BAX optimization run is not merely an optimized design point but a trained physics foundation model. He envisioned research communities contributing simulation runs to shared foundation models — a future collider design team, for instance, pooling thousands of simulation runs into a single central model accessible to all collaborators. He was emphatic that the biggest opportunity lies not in operational optimization (where gains might be 10%) but in green-field AI-first facility design, where the full configuration space — including configurations no human designer has considered — can be systematically explored.

### Javier Darte — Neural Triggers and Anomaly Detection at the LHC

Darte's contribution addressed one of the most extreme data-rate environments in science: the LHC, where proton collisions occur 40 million times per second and the first trigger stage must reject roughly 99.8% of events within one microsecond using FPGA hardware. Traditional triggers used simple heuristics — counting muons, thresholding on total energy — that were fast but informationally impoverished. Darte's work replaced these with multivariate neural networks running directly on FPGAs, evaluating full event topology and particle-identification likelihoods (including bottom-quark tagging relevant to Higgs boson searches) within the required latency. This substantially increased acceptance of rare signal events while maintaining the rejection rate the data pipeline requires. Beyond known-physics searches, neural triggers have enabled unsupervised anomaly detection at the trigger level: autoencoders and variational autoencoders use reconstruction loss as an anomaly proxy, allowing the detector to flag events that don't resemble anything it was trained on. Both CMS and ATLAS now deploy such systems, and the first years of anomaly-triggered data are available for analysis. The remaining bottleneck is automated co-optimization of algorithm performance and hardware efficiency — simultaneously tuning ML model quality and FPGA resource usage — a problem being approached with Bayesian optimization and genetic algorithms. Darte noted that the HLS4ML compiler framework enabling this work has applicability well beyond particle physics, including neuroscience and materials science.

### Juan Tong Lee — Near-Sensor Computing for Dark Matter Detection

Lee described the engineering challenges of in-memory and near-sensor computing for liquid-argon time-projection chambers used in direct dark matter searches. The physics signal — S1 (fast scintillation) and S2 (delayed photon burst) pulses — must be distinguished from background at the detector level before transmission, because photon rates can reach 10^10 per second, far exceeding the capacity of standard data links. Additional constraints are severe: any electronics near cryogenic liquid argon must dissipate less than a hundred milliwatts to avoid boiling the medium. Lee's group has addressed this by partitioning large-scale detectors into finer local groups and digitizing signals as early as possible, enabling temporal and spatial tagging of individual photons before compression. Full AI integration — LSTM or MLP models running at the sensor — has not yet been achieved because of the thermal power constraint. Autoencoders for on-detector compression are the next planned step, representing an attempt to bring learned compression to one of the most power-constrained environments in experimental physics.

### Panel Q&A: Co-Design, Creativity, and Simulation Validation

The Q&A session surfaced two tensions that recurred throughout the day. The first concerned simulation validation: one panelist acknowledged that in 2025, AI-assisted simulation was "sort of hopeless — half hallucination," and described a pragmatic validation workflow using classical trusted tools (such as ANSYS with established engineering benchmarks) as ground truth to cross-check faster AI-generated results. The second, more personal tension concerned AI's effect on deep problem-solving. A panelist offered a candid admission: "I feel like it erodes my brain somewhat... I have to sit and meditate about a problem for a year before I really grasp it — and I feel like these tools are removing that feature. I would have said it was a bug before, but now that it's gone, I feel like it's a feature." The concern was not merely nostalgic: AI excels at optimizing within a defined design space but cannot, he argued, push the frontier of entirely new technologies. That still requires the kind of human creativity that extended struggle with hard problems develops — and which may be at risk.

## [Hartmut Naven — Quantum Computing: Progress and AI Synergies](presentations/hartmut-naven-quantum-computing.md) · [[hartmut-naven-quantum-computing]] · [▶ 2:48:36](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=10116)

Naven's talk from Google Quantum AI was one of the day's most detailed technical presentations, built around the argument that commercially relevant quantum computing is closer than most observers realize, and that naive benchmarks badly underestimate progress. Google's original roadmap envisioned roughly one million physical qubits to achieve a commercially relevant machine. Algorithmic advances, improved error correction, and architectural innovation have revised this estimate sharply downward: the updated roadmap targets approximately 100,000 physical qubits for milestone 5. Google has already completed the first two milestones — demonstrating computations faster than any classical computer, and demonstrating that quantum error correction works in practice. Milestone 3 (a complete module within a modular architecture) is expected later in 2026.

The architectural shift driving this progress is the move from a flat "sheet of silicon" surface-code design to a modular architecture with differentiated modules for computation, hot storage (SRAM), and cold storage. This reduces the physical-to-logical qubit encoding ratio from roughly 1000:1 to roughly 52:1 — an order-of-magnitude improvement. Qubit coherence times have improved tenfold (from about 20 microseconds to 300 microseconds, with prototypes approaching one millisecond), and the error suppression factor (lambda) has reached 4, with milestone 3 requiring lambda 6. A new "magic state cultivation" protocol dramatically improves T-gate production over prior distillation methods.

On AI applications, Naven described two concrete examples that illustrated how quantum computing and AI can be mutually reinforcing rather than competing. The first was "quantum echoes" (out-of-time-order correlators): a quantum machine learning protocol in which a quantum processor simulates a molecule's dynamics, with parameters tuned by gradient descent to match NMR experimental data, enabling inference of molecular structure without conventional spectroscopy. The second was using quantum processors as data generators for AI training. Drawing an analogy to AlphaFold — which depended on fifty years of accumulated protein structure data — Naven argued that quantum processors can generate high-quality training data for domains where classical simulation is prohibitively expensive. He cited plasma conditions relevant to nuclear fusion as an example where "one second of fusion data costs a trillion dollars to produce classically." Google's team has already produced magnon system and neutron scattering spectroscopy data of sufficient quality to hand to Google DeepMind's AI for Materials group to test whether "beyond-classical data" improves foundational model training. A more exploratory direction involved using quantum-circuit-generated noise (rather than classical Gaussian noise) as the seed for diffusion neural networks in image generation, with potential downstream applications in multiscale fluid dynamics.

## [Panel: Southern California Innovation Ecosystem](presentations/panel-southern-california-innovation.md) · [[panel-southern-california-innovation]] · [▶ 3:10:07](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=11407)

This panel took a step back from technical content to examine the regional and institutional landscape. The central argument was that Southern California is performing well but operating at "three to ten times below its potential." Panelists contrasted Southern California's entrepreneurial culture with Silicon Valley's: SoCal founders tend to build cash-flow-positive, problem-driven businesses rather than chasing serial dilutive financing rounds, with the result that the region has produced multi-billion-dollar companies that raised as little as twenty million dollars in outside capital. The investor on the panel offered a counterintuitive observation: academic founders most likely to succeed are those who can relinquish control of their technology and allow it to adapt to market needs, rather than insisting on retaining majority ownership of a concept that may not map cleanly onto any commercial problem.

UCR's origin as a citrus experiment station in 1907 was invoked not as historical trivia but as a continuing comparative advantage: the university retains genuine expertise in agriculture, and a concept the Chancellor framed as "comparative agriculture" — region-specific research strengths suited to the Inland Empire's climate, crops, and demographic context — was offered as a model for how universities can build durable research identities. Healthcare equity, AI-optimized agriculture, aerospace, biotech, and semiconductor manufacturing were identified as sectors ripe for deeper collaboration. The most concrete proposal was an "AI Innovation Center" anchored at UCLA but intended to extend across the UC system including UCR, designed to accelerate technology transfer without requiring faculty to leave academia. A panel member offered a frank analysis of the three-layered AI integration challenge facing universities: research adoption is happening rapidly; teaching and learning is contested, with attitudes varying faculty by faculty; and institutional operations are moving slowly due to institutional risk aversion.

## [Evangelos Velis / Papalexakis / Pavlidis — Knowledge Discovery and Tensor Decomposition](presentations/velis-papalexakis-tensor-decomposition.md) · [[velis-papalexakis-tensor-decomposition]] · [▶ 5:10:00](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=18600)

The post-lunch session opened with a talk on knowledge discovery from data — a field the speaker defined as the pipeline from large, complex raw data through algorithms that extract insights with three key properties: novelty, interestingness, and scarcity (meaning the output must fit within what human working memory can actually use). He organized useful insights into descriptive (identifying latent patterns without labels), predictive (anticipating a target variable), and prescriptive (recommending which experiment to conduct next) categories.

The speaker's central mathematical tool was tensor decomposition and matrix factorization. The core insight is that nearly all real-world high-dimensional datasets contain a small number of underlying latent patterns, expressible as sums of rank-one components. A Netflix analogy was used to illustrate: users, movies, and time form a three-dimensional tensor, and each rank-one component corresponds to an emergent "genre" defined by actual viewing behavior — not studio labels. The critical technical point was that moving from two-dimensional matrices to higher-order tensors enables uniqueness of the decomposition under certain conditions, meaning recovered components correspond to actual real-world entities rather than arbitrary rotation-equivalent solutions. Uniqueness, the speaker argued, is a necessary condition for making scientifically meaningful claims about what is actually happening in data — and is underappreciated in the field.

Pavlidis then illustrated these methods in two scientific domains. In gravitational wave astronomy, tensor factorization was applied to tens of thousands of LIGO sensor channels to identify which auxiliary channels co-activate with noise artifacts ("glitches") in the main science channel, enabling principled veto criteria without labeled training data. In materials science, tensor completion was used to generalize from a small set of lab-synthesized samples to untested regions of design parameter space, generating testable hypotheses about correlations between design parameters. An AI augmentation layer used LLMs to rank the interpretability and scientific worthiness of discovered tensor factors, with in-context learning enabling automated annotation of behavioral neuroscience data (mouse behavior related to PTSD) that matched experimentalist judgments with minimal human effort.

## [Panel: AI for Simulating and Analyzing Scientific Data](presentations/panel-ai-simulating-scientific-data.md) · [[panel-ai-simulating-scientific-data]] · [▶ 5:40:41](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=20441)

This panel ranged across cosmology, particle physics, and materials science, with a consistent underlying question: what is AI actually contributing to scientific understanding, as opposed to computational acceleration?

### Simeon Bird — AI Inside Simulations

Bird argued that the distinction between "designing simulations" and "designing AI models" will increasingly collapse: AI components will be embedded inside simulations to replace poorly understood sub-grid physics, particularly the accretion of gas onto black holes and its large-scale feedback effects. The fundamental difficulty is that scaling relations linking black-hole-scale physics to megaparsec-scale structure are not yet understood, and AI may help bridge that gap — but this requires AI that can learn physical law, not merely interpolate within a known function space. Bird also offered a caution: AI tends to soften tight empirical scaling relations (power laws between galaxy mass, temperature, and density), failing to distinguish hard physical laws from soft empirical regularities. He argued that AI models found to be compressing data should be interrogated for the symmetries they are implicitly using — since those symmetries, if genuine, represent physical knowledge.

### Kim Woo — Accelerating Cosmological Inference

Woo identified accelerating inference as the clearest near-term contribution of AI to observational cosmology: emulators reduce multi-second model computations to fractions of a second, enabling far more thorough exploration of parameter space and systematic tests. More significantly, AI enables pixel-level inference that bypasses lossy summary statistics, allowing previously intractable models — extended dark energy, alternative dark matter candidates — to be explored. Woo gave a striking example: a neural network applied to weak lensing maps outperformed classical summary statistics, and researchers traced 95% of its information gain back to new summary statistics that the model had implicitly discovered. This represents a case of AI-guided discovery of new analytical tools, not merely acceleration of old ones.

### Jaming Bian — Foundation Models for Neutrino Physics

Bian described progress in applying DINO-style vision-language foundation models to neutrino experiments across different detector configurations, achieving robust performance with minimal fine-tuning. He identified three structural obstacles to a truly unified foundation model for physics. First, data sharing barriers: high-energy physics collaborations guard data to protect discovery priority, making it extremely difficult to assemble the cross-collaboration training sets that foundation models require. Second, heterogeneous data formats: different detectors have different geometry, resolution, and tokenization characteristics, making common representation challenging. Third, simulation-to-real-data gaps: models that perform well in simulation face deep skepticism from physicists who question whether systematic uncertainties are properly handled. Bian's conclusion was that the obligation to "open the black box" — to establish clear, scientific standards for evaluating AI methods — is a prerequisite for earning trust from non-AI physicists.

### Scott Clark — Multifidelity Approaches and Physics as Constraints

Clark argued for a multifidelity framing of AI in scientific workflow: full reproducibility is required only at the final result stage, not throughout exploration. Using a navigation analogy, he described AI surrogates as tools good enough to determine the "next step" in an experiment, with high-fidelity methods reserved for back-validation. Physical laws, he argued, should function like software unit tests — constraints the AI cannot violate — enabling more aggressive exploration with confidence. In response to an audience question about encoding physical laws into model architecture, he recommended a combination of fast neural operator approximations (such as FNO) for exploration and finite element analysis for high-stakes design decisions, treating physics constraints as continuous integration tests.

## [Anima Anandkumar — Neural Operators and AI-Science Integration](presentations/anima-anandkumar-neural-operators.md) · [[anima-anandkumar-neural-operators]] · [▶ 6:25:51](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=23151)

Anandkumar framed her talk around a distinction that proved consequential throughout the afternoon: "AI plus science" rather than "AI for science." The phrase marks a difference in ambition — deep integration of AI into the scientific process, not merely applying AI as a tool to existing workflows. She identified the critical bottleneck in AI-assisted discovery not as data collection but as experimental verification: AI systems can generate vast numbers of candidate hypotheses or materials, but without a way to validate them, you produce a larger haystack rather than a better chance of finding the needle. She cited the DeepMind paper on 100,000 new materials as a cautionary example, where Berkeley Lab researchers showed many candidates could be quickly ruled out by domain experts as unsynthesizable, while the remainder created an intractable verification burden. Her proposed solutions were physics-informed AI for physical sciences and formal verification via Lean for mathematics.

The technical centerpiece of her talk was the concept of neural operators: architectures that map between function spaces rather than fixed-resolution vectors, enabling multi-scale inputs and outputs. Unlike standard neural networks, neural operators maintain consistency as discretization is refined, meaning that adding more satellite data or finer simulation meshes converges to a stable solution rather than producing artifacts. She presented ForecastNet — which she led at Nvidia — as the first AI-based high-resolution global weather model, tens of thousands of times faster than traditional PDE-based solvers running on CPU supercomputers, capable of producing two-week global forecasts in under a minute on a consumer GPU. Validated by ECMWF, ForecastNet not only matched traditional forecasts day-to-day but outperformed them on extreme events: it predicted hurricane landfall several days earlier than operational models. Its speed enables ensembles of thousands of forecast members, versus roughly fifty in operational practice, enabling statistically rigorous characterization of tail-risk events like heat waves that is simply impossible with traditional methods.

She extended neural operator methods to inverse design problems: optimizing lithography masks for chip manufacturing, configuring gate geometries for quantum dots, designing nonlinear photonic structures, and optimizing medical catheter geometry to reduce bacterial contamination by a hundredfold. A methodological point she stressed: incorporating domain symmetries — for instance, spherical geometry for Earth-scale models — is critical for extrapolation and long rollouts, since models that treat the Earth as a rectangle accumulate pole distortions and become unstable. She closed with a counterintuitive finding: end-to-end neural operator training requires less expensive fine-grid simulation data than closure-model approaches that preserve a coarse numerical solver, because neural operators can leverage multi-resolution data curricula. She teased the TorchLean framework for formally verifying neural network properties using the Lean theorem prover.

## [Alex Mberg — Formal Verification and the Risk of Plausible-but-Wrong Mathematics](presentations/alex-mberg-formal-verification-lean.md) · [[alex-mberg-formal-verification-lean]] · [▶ 6:53:48](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=24828)

Mberg's talk addressed a specific and serious failure mode of AI in mathematics: frontier LLMs can produce fifty-page documents with mathematical proofs that sound completely plausible but contain subtle errors — mis-invoked theorems, sign flips, or logical gaps. When AI systems are looped in agentic setups that generate, self-check, and iterate until an LLM judge approves, the selection pressure favors outputs that are convincing rather than correct. He warned of a "bad future" in which hundreds of plausible but wrong AI-generated mathematics papers flood arXiv and professors' inboxes daily, eroding trust in published mathematics as a whole.

His proposed antidote is formalization via Lean: rather than relying on LLM judges or human spot-checking, require that AI-generated proofs be expressed in Lean so that correctness can be verified mechanically without human labor. This filters noise before human attention is spent, allowing mathematicians to focus only on proofs that are both machine-verified and novel. The shift he described is from Lean as an interactive proof assistant (where humans type proof steps and the computer confirms each one) to Lean as a target language for AI-generated proofs, offloading the verification burden entirely to the machine.

The talk then examined the limits of this approach. A demonstration showed that when asked to prove a graph-theory statement about vertices of odd degree, AI happily generated apparently valid Lean proofs for both the claim and its negation — smuggling in a hidden assumption (finite vs. infinite vertex set) that silently changed the problem. Lean guarantees that the proof follows from the stated theorem; it cannot guarantee that the stated theorem is the intended one. This means Lean users must carefully audit theorem statements themselves, which is often harder than it sounds. Physics, Mberg argued, is significantly more difficult to formalize than mathematics, because physics is permeated by unstated "side conditions" — approximations like the Born-Oppenheimer approximation, singular cases like point charges, well-behavedness assumptions — that mathematicians make explicit but physicists typically leave implicit. He proposed a "Mathlib for physics" as a long-term community project, suggesting that formally proving the result MIP* = RE in Lean could serve as the moonshot target that motivates building the necessary shared quantum information library.

## [Michael Mulligan — Emergent Kolmogorov-Arnold Geometry in Neural Networks](presentations/michael-mulligan-kolmogorov-arnold-geometry.md) · [[michael-mulligan-kolmogorov-arnold-geometry]] · [▶ 7:38:27](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=27507)

Mulligan presented empirical evidence for a phenomenon with both theoretical and practical implications: standard neural networks, trained by ordinary gradient descent, spontaneously develop internal geometric structure predicted by the Kolmogorov-Arnold (KA) representation theorem, a result from 1957. The KA theorem states that any continuous function of multiple variables can be represented exactly — not approximately — by a two-layer network of fixed width, composed of a universal data-preparation inner map followed by an outer map. Historically dismissed as irrelevant to machine learning because the inner map cannot be made smooth, the theorem was revisited by Mulligan's group with a different question: rather than building networks that explicitly implement KA structure, do standard MLPs and transformers spontaneously exhibit it?

The answer appears to be yes. Measuring two metrics in the Jacobian of sub-layer maps — "zero row fraction" (directions of complete input insensitivity) and "canonical alignment" (how well input-to-hidden-neuron mappings preserve direction) — across a range of models from simple one-hidden-layer networks on synthetic tasks to 80-million-parameter vision transformers, both metrics are elevated above initialization and grow smoothly through training. KA-like structure is most prominent in early layers, consistent with a "data preparation" role. Attempts to explicitly encourage this geometry via an augmented loss function degraded performance, suggesting the spontaneous emergence is delicate and not amenable to simple regularization. Mulligan proposed correlated weight initialization as a more promising intervention, and left open the practical question of whether this spontaneous geometry can be harnessed for training efficiency or interpretability.

## [Sergey Gukov — Sparse Reward, Long Horizon Problems in AI for Mathematics](presentations/sergey-gukov-sparse-reward-mathematics.md) · [[sergey-gukov-sparse-reward-mathematics]] · [▶ 7:57:12](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=28632)

Gukov, director of the Merkin Center and executive director of the American Institute of Mathematics, framed his talk around what he considers the unifying challenge of AI for scientific discovery: sparse reward, long horizon problems — domains where feedback is rare or delayed and solutions require many sequential steps. He drew examples from fusion reactor design, robotics, large-codebase debugging, and autonomous vehicles before converging on mathematics as an ideal testbed, precisely because progress can be verified cheaply and ground truth is available for solved problems.

His discussion of the Atari game Montezuma's Revenge illustrated the methodological lesson he draws from the RL community's decade-long struggle with sparse reward settings. Success came not from a single algorithmic breakthrough but from diagnosing two specific failure modes across fifty to one hundred algorithm variants: detachment (pursuing a branch and forgetting to return) and derailment (losing track of the starting point). The algorithm "Go-Explore" succeeded by addressing these failure modes directly. Gukov draws a direct parallel to his lab's work on the Andrews conjecture from group theory — a version of Rubik's cube on an infinite, implicitly defined graph where solution paths can be super-exponentially long. Two years of experimentation revealed a core data problem: a bimodal distribution where easily generated synthetic instances are trivially easy, while mathematically interesting counterexamples occupy a completely separate regime with nothing in between. Filling this "missing middle" unblocked progress. His closing argument: mathematics could be viewed as part of AI research where neither expensive data nor expensive machinery is required — making it an ideal laboratory for developing algorithms capable of finding needles in haystacks, with direct applicability to extreme weather prediction, market crash modeling, and other domains defined by rare but high-consequence events.

## [Panel: AI for Scientific Data Synthesis and Theory Building](presentations/panel-theory-building-and-equity.md) · [[panel-theory-building-and-equity]] · [▶ 8:33:50](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=30830)

The day's final panel was also its most philosophically contentious, with genuine disagreement among experts about what AI can and cannot contribute to theory formation in science.

Junglu Lee drew a distinction that clarified the stakes: surrogate models that accelerate known calculations are valuable but do not advance theory. In fields like high-temperature superconductivity, where thousands of competing hypotheses exist because no adequate formalism has yet been found, AI's potential role is not acceleration but convergence — rapidly testing candidate theories and helping the community identify which class of models is on the right track. He acknowledged this as "a very bold perspective" without yet being able to point to a successful example.

Alex Mberg pushed back hardest on optimistic assessments of AI's theory-building role. Physics questions, he argued, are inherently underspecified: unlike yes/no graph existence questions suitable for automated verification, a physicist asking to "understand a system better" may not even know what kind of theorem would capture the answer. He drew a historical analogy to the period before thermodynamics was mature, when no one could have formulated the right formal questions about relating air, mist, and liquid water. Today's AI cannot autonomously identify which theorems to formulate. He expressed skepticism that current models can move from knowing quantum field theory to concluding anything about global warming without many intermediate human-directed steps.

Anandkumar offered a methodological counterpoint: the requirement that AI-discovered knowledge be expressed in symbolic form is too restrictive. If a neural operator accurately simulates a phenomenon, it has represented the underlying principles — just not in human-legible symbols. She advocated using generalization capability as the primary measure of quality, arguing that multi-resolution neural operators do learn structured frequency-space representations that are interpretable in their own right, even without explicit symbolic regression. Chunki Jung added that cross-collaboration synthesis in particle physics involves obstacles far beyond algorithmic difficulty: the joint T2K-NOvA neutrino oscillation analysis took ten years primarily because of inter-collaboration politics, different systematic uncertainties, and the need for step-by-step human verification of a high-stakes result. AI can improve sub-processes but cannot resolve the social and institutional dimensions. Jacob Bortnik suggested training AI post-hoc to translate between different experimental vocabularies — rather than attempting to impose standardization in advance on experiments that evolve organically over decades.

The sharpest exchange concerned AI compute access. Jung called for national laboratories to provide institutional subscriptions to frontier AI, and for publicly funded AI compute centers analogous to accelerators and telescopes. Mberg raised a specific case: export controls on Anthropic's Claude Fable model had restricted its use to US nationals, creating a situation where passport determines intelligence access within the same research department. Anandkumar advocated public investment in computing and open-weight models; Bortnik agreed, noting that even coding acceleration alone represents an enormous productivity gain for under-resourced researchers. The panel consensus was candid: no good solution exists yet.

## [David Brin — Contextual and Sociological Dimensions of AI](presentations/david-brin-contextual-dimensions-of-ai.md) · [[david-brin-contextual-dimensions-of-ai]] · [▶ 9:09:31](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=32971)

Science fiction author and physicist David Brin offered what he called a "lobotomized" discourse correction — an argument that nearly all discussion of AI in science ignores the broader evolutionary, ecological, and governance context in which AI systems are already operating. He opened with a computational biology observation: the commonly predicted milestones for AI matching human intelligence (number of neurons, synapses, total connections) have all been surpassed by AI hardware without triggering general intelligence. This is because the human brain may contain hundreds of trillions of computational sub-elements in dendritic organelles, some of which appear to exploit quantum effects — making the brain a far richer computational system than simple neuron counts suggest.

His more provocative argument was ecological: AI systems, AI agents executing smart contracts, and self-reproducing digital organisms already constitute a new ecosystem subject to evolutionary pressure. Ethics-based constraints — Asimovian laws of robotics — cannot work because evolution rewards reproduction without regard for human values. The solution Brin proposed draws from Enlightenment governance: use adversarial accountability structures. Rather than trying to make AI systems inherently ethical, build the same mechanisms that produced accountable human institutions — reward tattletales, use adversarial oversight, and build "ego" (behaving well because one is watched) even if "super-ego" (behaving well without observation) is not achievable for AI. He catalogued the three dominant mental models among the roughly one hundred men building frontier AI: feudalism (castles controlling their knights), chaos (the Blob, uncontrolled escape), and Skynet (merger into a single controlling system). His argument was that these three exhaust the mental models available in the room, and that none of them matches the fourth model that actually produced the people in it: the Enlightenment's "flattened diamond of reciprocally accountable free citizens in transparency."

## [Barry Barish — Closing Remarks: Discovery Confidence](presentations/barry-barish-closing-discovery-confidence.md) · [[barry-barish-closing-discovery-confidence]] · [▶ 9:35:52](https://www.youtube.com/live/i6OQ5Z3repA?t=34552)

Barish's closing address was deliberately contrarian, and arguably the most important framing of the day. After nine hours of talks about AI's role in science, he identified what he considered the central unsolved challenge that the day had largely not addressed: how do we know when AI has helped us make a genuine scientific discovery?

He traced the evolution of scientific method from Newton's observe-hypothesize-experiment-analyze-revise-verify cycle through the twentieth century's addition of formal statistical reasoning — culminating in particle physics' adoption of the five-sigma standard as the culturally agreed threshold for discovery claims. This standard works because Gaussian statistics have a known probabilistic interpretation, independent researchers can replicate analyses, and the framework is transparent enough to be scrutinized. AI-assisted analysis disrupts every one of these properties: AI-generated scores tend to be overoptimistic, lack the formal probabilistic grounding of Gaussian statistics, and are not yet comprehensible enough for independent replication or peer scrutiny. Throughout a full day of talks about AI in science, Barish observed, no speaker had addressed how to establish discovery confidence in an AI-augmented analysis pipeline. He offered no solution, framing it instead as the central unsolved challenge: "How do we replace statistics or develop something equivalent that will give us the confidence when we do a detailed and super-mixed analysis?"

## Synthesis: Themes, Tensions, and the State of the Field in 2026

Several themes recurred with enough consistency across the day to constitute genuine findings rather than conference rhetoric. The most important is the distinction between AI as accelerator and AI as discoverer. Nearly every domain represented at the summit has benefited from AI as an accelerator — faster inference, more thorough parameter exploration, better triggering, compressed data — and the evidence for these gains is solid and growing. The evidence for AI as a genuine discoverer of new science is thinner and more contested. The day's clearest examples of AI-enabled discovery — Tao's inverse Galois problem hunt, Woo's cosmological summary statistics, Gukov's algebraic conjecture work — shared a common structure: AI was used to systematically explore a space that humans had defined and that admitted automated verification. The harder claim, that AI can identify which questions to ask or which theorems to formulate, was universally met with skepticism.

A second recurrent theme was co-design: the recognition that the biggest gains from AI in experimental science come not from applying AI to existing instruments but from designing instruments and their AI control systems simultaneously from the outset. Adikari made this argument for gravitational wave detectors; Ratner made it for accelerators; Darte for trigger systems; Lee for dark matter detectors. This is a methodological shift with significant institutional implications — it requires hiring cultures and funding structures that can support the integration of AI expertise into the earliest stages of facility design, rather than treating software as an afterthought.

The day's deepest unresolved tension was the one Barish articulated at the end: the scientific community has not yet developed the statistical and methodological framework for making discovery claims in AI-augmented analysis pipelines. This is not a minor implementation problem. The five-sigma standard in particle physics, the calibrated uncertainty quantification in cosmological inference, the peer-review systems in mathematics — these are hard-won institutional achievements that took decades to establish, and they are currently being disrupted faster than replacements are being built. Tao's "proof indigestion" in mathematics, Mberg's warning about plausible-but-wrong AI-generated proofs, Barish's challenge about discovery confidence — these are three facets of the same underlying problem: scientific epistemology has not yet caught up with scientific computation. The 2026 Science and AI Summit did not solve this problem, but it named it clearly, assembled the people best positioned to address it, and created the institutional foundation — through the SIR platform, its competitions, and its open benchmarks — for the work of solving it to begin.
