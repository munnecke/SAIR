One of the most frequent questions people have when seeing Mosaic and the World Wide Web is, “Who is in charge here?”  [People expect some hierarchical power structure that is responsible for the Web, analogous to the way that a CEO or government official is at the top of an organizational pyramid]{#ww-pyramid-expectation .claim tense=indexical status=open}.

The pyramid is an extremely stable structure, as can be seen by the ancient pyramids of Egypt or Mexico. The weight of the top block is pressed upon the next lower level, which spreads out among ever increasing hierarchical levels.  [The pyramid is a spectacular design if you want to build stability, but is severely limiting if you want to build flexible, adaptive structures]{#ww-pyramid-stability-tradeoff .interpretation}.

Many of our organizations mimic the rigid hierarchy of the pyramid.  Government agencies, corporations, churches, universities, and the military all have evolved hierarchical structures that serve to maintain their stability.  In many cases, this is a very good thing.  I am glad that the trigger on our nuclear arsenal is a controlled by a very rigid command and control hierarchy.  In periods of rapid change, however, the stability of the hierarchy can work against the organization.

In the 1980’s, companies which offered a complete line of  goods and services dominated the computer industry.  IBM, Digital, and Wang each offered a vertical stack of technologies that were generally compatible within their corporate family, not with each other.  They sold central processing units, operating systems, languages, applications, and consulting services.  They built their organizational hierarchies about this business model.

Today the industry has changed dramatically, separated into open, competitive layers.  At the local computer store, you can buy a computer based on an Intel central processor inside a Compaq computer, and running a Microsoft operating system.  The pyramids of Wang, Digital, and IBM were tilted because their foundations in the market place eroded.  Intel, Compaq, and Microsoft have thrived by growing and adapting to their customer’s needs.

For better or worse, the transition from these stovepipe organizations of old to the infrastructure market of the computer industry of today is a harbinger of things to come. [Organizations that rely on their historic position based on the size, stability, or market dominance may find that their pyramids will tilt beneath them]{#ww-pyramid-tilt-forecast .hypothesis status=open}.

Of course, young, idealistic reformers who don’t accept the established order may tilt pyramids in Quixotic quests.  Steve Jobs’ introduction of the Macintosh at the 1984 Superbowl was one of the more dramatic of these efforts.  Within every organization, there are people who see the need to change their way of doing business to adapt to their changing environment. Typically, they are frustrated by the hierarchy surrounding them.

This is particularly true in government, where stability may be more important than the accomplishment of the organization’s mission.  Those who seek to change the organization frequently encounter intense resitance from the hierachy.

I speak from experience in this matter.  In the late 1970’s, I turned down an opportunity to be one of the second dozen employees at Apple Computer, and went to work for the Veteran’s Administration.  I was part of a group of people around the country hired to develop a mini-computer based hospital information system.  [Although this system was quite successful (about 200,000 people use it each day), it was a very radical concept at the time, and there was great internal political uproar in the matter]{#ww-pyramid-vista .historical_claim}.  [As one of the leaders of this “underground railroad”, I was downgraded a GS level in the civil service system]{#ww-pyramid-gs-downgrade .historical_claim}.  There was nothing the personnel regulations to cope with those who work laterally across the organization instead of hierarchically. Employees responsible for a fixed amount of work within the hierarchy could only be promoted by figuring out a way of hiring more people to build the pyramid.

The World Wide Web is an infrastructure that supports the adaptive, rapidly changing organization we need to cope with our changing environment. There is no single world view, no single pyramid from which we can confidently say, "This is the top and everything else is subordinate to this point of view.”  [On the Internet, there are no equivalents to the television network programmers who decide content]{#ww-pyramid-no-programmers .claim tense=indexical status=open}.  The web is capable of taking many shapes (including hierarchical), and may be tailored to specific needs without loss of integrity.

In contrast to the hierarchy, each user of the web is in control, viewing the world from their own perspective.  Who is really in charge of the Internet?  You are.

::: {#ww-pyramid-hierarchy-open .unknowledge status=open asserted=2026-07-04 asserted_by=claude-curator parents="ww-pyramid-tilt-forecast@motivated"}
Do network infrastructures durably flatten hierarchies, or do new hierarchies re-form on top of them? The permissionless publishing layer this column describes still exists in 2026, and heavy platform concentration sits above it. Both facts are stable; which one governs the experience of most users is the part that remains open.
:::

::: {#ww-pyramid-2026 .resolution asserted=2026-07-04 asserted_by=claude-curator outdates=ww-pyramid-no-programmers parents="ww-pyramid-no-programmers@motivated"}
This was an accurate description of 1994, and it did not stay true. By the mid-2010s most people reached the web through a few platforms whose recommendation systems perform the function of the television network programmer, deciding what each viewer sees next. The claim is outdated in the strict sense: it was right when written, and the world changed underneath it. The closing answer, "You are," is harder to score and gets no edge. At the 2026 Science and AI summit, David Brin argued for ecosystems of [reciprocally accountable agents]{ref=brin-enlightenment-accountability}, a flattened structure of mutual watching that resembles this column's tilted pyramid more than the feed-driven web that grew in between. The resemblance is recorded as prose only; no derivation edge is claimed.
:::
