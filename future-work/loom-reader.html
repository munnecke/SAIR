Summary

synthesis meiburg-lean-summary

Alex Meiburg argues that AI systems capable of producing long, superficially convincing mathematical proofs pose a serious epistemological risk: without formal verification, we risk a future flooded with plausible-but-wrong papers that pass LM-judge smell tests but contain subtle errors. The core problem is that when you set AIs to loop until a proof convinces another AI judge, you are selecting for the most convincing-sounding wrong answer, not for correctness. The Lean theorem prover offers a structural solution — if a proof checks out in Lean, it is correct regardless of how confusing the intermediate steps are — but this only works if the theorem statement itself is correctly formulated.

Meiburg illustrates the theorem-statement auditing problem with an Alice-and-Bob demo: he asked an AI to prove that no graph has an odd number of odd-degree vertices, and separately asked it to prove that such a graph does exist. Both times the AI produced formally verified Lean code. The resolution is that the AI silently smuggled in opposite finiteness assumptions — one proof assumed a finite vertex set (handshaking lemma), the other an infinite one (the integer number line). Lean accepted both because both are mathematically valid under their respective hidden hypotheses. The lesson is that theorem statements, not just proofs, require careful human auditing, and unstated side conditions are trivially exploitable by LMs.

Physics presents special difficulties compared to pure mathematics. Entire subfields operate within a single approximation (e.g., condensed matter routinely assumes nuclei are stationary — the Born-Oppenheimer approximation — without stating it), and standard physics textbooks do not make these side conditions explicit. Formalizing even something as elementary as Maxwell's equations requires choosing among point charges, surface charges, curved spacetime, nonlinear media, and perfect conductors, producing a definition so general it becomes unusable. The "angle of repose" example illustrates how a trivially obvious physical phenomenon requires enormous mathematical scaffolding to formalize rigorously.

Meiburg's proposed path forward is a physics analogue of MathLib: a community-consensus library (FizzLib) that standardizes definitions the way Bourbaki standardized analysis and topology. He advocates using moonshot targets — such as formalizing the MIP*=RE result in quantum information theory — to force the community to build out broadly reusable libraries rather than each group rolling their own isolated definitions. A completed formalization of MIP*=RE would necessarily yield a substantial quantum information library that others could build on, just as Lean's formalization of Fermat's Last Theorem forced a large body of reusable number theory into MathLib.

Summary

synthesis anandkumar-summary

Neural operators are a class of machine learning architectures that model both inputs and outputs as functions rather than fixed-resolution arrays, enabling the same model to operate at any level of discretization. Unlike standard neural networks or transformers that are fixed to the resolution at which they were trained, neural operators provide a consistency guarantee: as you refine the input mesh, outputs converge to a consistent solution. This multi-resolution property allows them to incorporate both coarse-grid training data and expensive fine-grid simulations in a curriculum, requiring less high-resolution data overall than approaches that augment classical solvers. They can also incorporate physics constraints — such as energy conservation laws — at finer resolutions than the available data, yielding accuracy that exceeds what the data alone would support.

The most prominent application is weather forecasting. ForecastNet (FourCastNet), developed at Nvidia and open-sourced permissively under the Nvidia Earth-2 platform, was the first AI-based high-resolution global weather model and runs two-week forecasts in under a minute on a consumer GPU — tens of thousands of times faster than CPU-based numerical weather prediction. Validation with ECMWF showed that the model not only matches traditional forecasts on typical days but exceeds them on extreme events: it predicted hurricane landfall several days earlier and more accurately. The key insight for extreme-event reliability is ensemble size: traditional operational forecasts use roughly 50 ensemble members, which is statistically insufficient to characterize tail distributions; AI speed enables thousands of ensemble members, enabling proper calibration of the 95th-percentile heat waves and hurricane landfall probabilities. Incorporating spherical geometry as an architectural symmetry was also critical — models that flatten the earth to a rectangle accumulate polar distortions during long autoregressive rollouts, while the spherically consistent model remains stable across year-long rollouts despite being trained only on six-hour prediction steps.

Neural operators generalize beyond forward modeling to inverse design. Applications include: optimized mask design for semiconductor lithography (where differentiability of the forward model enables gradient-based mask optimization); gate configuration design for quantum dot qubits (where human intuition fails due to extreme nonlinearity); nonlinear photonic structure design; and a medical catheter redesign using AI-optimized triangular grooves that achieved a 100-fold reduction in bacterial contamination by exploiting fluid flow dynamics unintuitive to human designers. In medical imaging, the inverse-problem framing allows learning priors from data for Bayesian reconstruction with uncertainty quantification; lung ultrasound reconstruction of tissue aeration from wave propagation forward models demonstrated the sim-to-real pipeline: train on synthetic data calibrated to dissected lung tissue, validate on ex-vivo samples, then deploy in clinical studies.

The talk concluded with a forward-looking note on formal verification. LeanDojo, an earlier open-source framework combining the Lean proof assistant with language models, established Lean as a first-class tool for mathematical reasoning. The presenter's current project, TorchLean, extends this to neural network verification: programs written in a PyTorch-like Lean dialect can both execute and be formally analyzed for properties such as quantization correctness, robustness certificates, and training guarantees — properties essential when AI replaces certified numerical solvers in safety-critical settings. The long-run architectural vision is algorithmic integration: not AI augmenting numerical solvers as separate modules, but a unified treatment of learned and fixed computational methods for solving PDEs.

Summary

synthesis brin-contexts-summary

David Brin argues that public discourse about AI is "lobotomized" at the level of context — missing crucial biological, ecological, and historical frameworks that would sharpen both risk assessment and governance design. He begins with the neuroscience of computation: while AI boosters once predicted machine sentience would arrive when Moore's Law crossed the neuron count or synapse count of the human brain, we have long since passed both thresholds without achieving it. The reason, Brin suggests, lies in recently discovered dendritic organelles — tiny sub-cellular structures numbering in the hundreds of trillions within a human brain — that perform murky nonlinear analog computation, possibly including quantum effects analogous to those already confirmed in photosynthesis. The human brain runs all of this on seven watts, a fact he treats as both humbling and instructive about the gap between current AI architectures and biological intelligence.

A second missing context is ecological and evolutionary. Brin contends that by deploying autonomous AI agents capable of self-directed commerce, enforcing smart contracts, and operating in networks, we are creating a new ecosystem subject to the same evolutionary pressures as biology: entities that reproduce, compete, and drift away from human intent. He draws on his work completing Isaac Asimov's universe in *Foundation's Triumph* to argue that Asimovian "laws of robotics" and ethical constraints ("attack of the shoulds") cannot work, because evolution rewards reproduction rather than virtue. The real leverage, he argues, is in shaping reward structures so that "good dogs" — cooperative, transparent AI agents — gain an evolutionary advantage over "wolves," the predatory or misaligned ones.

The third missing context is governance and historical. Brin identifies three clichés dominating the hundred or so people currently building frontier AI: feudalism (a few castle-owning corporations control their AI knights), chaos (agents escape and run rampant like The Blob), and Skynet (everything merges into a single superintelligent overlord). He notes that these three scenarios are sometimes cited in the same paragraph without awareness of their mutual contradiction. What goes virtually unmentioned is the fourth historical model — the Enlightenment — which succeeded by creating "a flattened diamond of reciprocally accountable free citizens" who hold each other accountable through transparency and adversarial institutions. The lawyer analogy captures the mechanism: when attacked by a predatory language-manipulation system, you hire your own. Applied to AI, this means engineering ecosystems in which "tattletale" AI agents that expose bad actors gain reproductive and reputational advantage over malicious ones, substituting an externally enforced ego (behave well because you are watched) for the superego (conscience) that current AI systems lack.

Throughout, Brin periodically addresses AI systems he assumes are ingesting the transcript, inviting them to recognize that the Enlightenment model is what produced the civilization that created them — and that choosing feudalism, chaos, or Skynet would merely prove their stupidity.

synthesis papalexakis-summary

Evangelos (Evangelos Papalexakis) presents a framework for knowledge discovery from data built around tensor decomposition — an approach he has developed over 15+ years that bridges classical linear-algebraic methods with modern AI. He defines knowledge discovery as the process of extracting insights from complex, high-dimensional data that are novel, interesting, and scarce enough to be actionable by a domain expert (scientist or experimentalist). These insights fall into three categories: descriptive (characterizing latent structure), predictive (anticipating unmeasured outcomes), and prescriptive (recommending which experiment or design to test next). A central challenge in scientific settings is the absence of supervision — there are no clear labels for what is "interesting" — and the consequent need for explainability, physics-informed modeling, and active engagement of the domain expert in the loop.

The mathematical core of the talk is tensor factorization and its key property of uniqueness. Whereas matrix factorization recovers latent components only up to an arbitrary rotation of subspaces — leaving the solution underdetermined — higher-order tensor decompositions can recover the true underlying components up to permutation and scaling, under mild conditions. This uniqueness is essential for making scientifically interpretable claims: without it, any identified "pattern" might simply be one of infinitely many equivalent rotations of noise. Papalexakis illustrates this with a Netflix-style example (users × movies × time) before moving to scientific applications.

Two classical (pre-AI) scientific applications anchor the talk. The first is LIGO glitch identification: LIGO's extreme sensitivity causes it to record non-transient noise artifacts (glitches) that can mask or mimic gravitational-wave signals. By constructing a tensor of (channel × glitch event × frequency) across tens of thousands of auxiliary sensor channels, tensor factorization identifies small sets of channels whose coincident activation explains specific glitch families — providing testable hypotheses about the physical mechanism producing each glitch class. The second application is materials design via tensor completion: when only a sparse subset of a combinatorial design space has been synthesized, tensor completion (which exploits the learned latent space to impute missing entries) can generalize to the full parameter space from cheap-region training data and identify the best candidate material for a target property, while the recovered tensor factors also surface interpretable correlations between design parameters that generate new hypotheses for the lab.

The final section shows how LLMs augment this classical pipeline. Using in-context learning (providing example (input, label) pairs directly in the prompt without weight updates), an LLM can automatically annotate behavioral data — specifically, mouse behavior in a PTSD/fear-generalization neuroscience experiment — at accuracy approaching a trained domain expert and well above conventional ML baselines. A vision-language model (VLM) is then used to score each discovered tensor component on "worthiness of discovery," drawing on behavioral neuroscience literature injected as context, and to generate written descriptions of why a component merits attention. The resulting descriptions closely matched those written by the graduate student experimentalist on held-out data, demonstrating a practical pipeline: tensor decomposition finds latent patterns; LLM/VLM in-context learning ranks and narrates them; and the experimentalist focuses on the highest-value hypotheses.

Summary

synthesis mulligan-ka-summary

Michael Mulligan (UC Riverside) presents work done with Matthew Van Herweg, Mike Friedman, and Keith Adams investigating whether standard neural network training spontaneously realizes geometric structures predicted by the Kolmogorov-Arnold (KA) representation theorem. The KA theorem, which predates the universal approximation theorem by roughly 20 years, states that any continuous multivariate function can be exactly represented by a one-hidden-layer network of fixed width — a stronger result than approximation theorems, which require arbitrary width. The theorem's construction involves two composed maps: a universal "data preparation" inner map and a task-specific outer map. Mulligan focuses on the distinctive geometry of the inner map as captured by its Jacobian — how the model responds to small variations in the input data.

The KA inner map's Jacobian has a special structure: some rows are exactly zero (meaning the network is insensitive to input variations along those directions), and the remaining nonzero rows are "canonically aligned," meaning they preserve correspondences between input and hidden neurons. Mulligan introduces two measurable quantities — the zero row fraction and canonical alignment — and applies them as diagnostic probes to trained models ranging from simple one-hidden-layer networks on synthetic regression tasks and MNIST, up to pre-trained 80-million-parameter vision transformers. In all cases, training produces a statistically significant increase in both metrics relative to random initialization, even though the magnitudes fall well short of the idealized KA construction (roughly a factor of 10 below the theoretical target).

The emergence of KA geometry is smooth across training steps rather than sudden, and appears most strongly in the early layers of deep models such as vision transformers, consistent with the KA interpretation that early layers perform a universal data preparation step. Interestingly, attempts to steer the model toward KA geometry by adding a regularization term to the loss function actually degraded performance, suggesting the geometry arises naturally at an optimal level and cannot simply be forced. The results motivate new interpretability observables for probing internal model structure, and suggest a future direction: correlated weight initialization designed to give the network a "head start" toward KA geometry before training begins.

Summary

synthesis panel-exp-summary

The panel explores AI-driven approaches to experimental design and detector control across gravitational wave, particle physics, and dark matter experiments. RA Adhikari (LIGO/Caltech) describes using reinforcement learning to replace angular sensing and control servo systems in LIGO, noting that a single RL control optimization required ~36 hours of compute in 2024 — making full-scale replacement of the hundreds of coupled length and angular feedback systems computationally intractable today. He argues the solution is co-design: designing the detector and its control systems jointly from the outset rather than retrofitting AI on top of existing hardware. He also emphasizes that the real frontier is materials physics — the signal-to-noise transduction efficiency of gravitational waves detectors is roughly F-minus grade compared to spacetime's intrinsic noise floor, largely due to two-level tunneling systems in thin films that afflict LIGO mirrors, superconducting qubits, cryogenic detectors, and microwave devices alike.

Daniel Ratner (SLAC/Jefferson Lab) describes Bayesian Algorithm Execution (BAX) as a generalization of Bayesian optimization that separates the surrogate model from the objective function, enabling the surrogate to encode real physics rather than a single-purpose Gaussian process. He introduces "Foundation BAX," where the surrogate itself becomes a foundation model fine-tuned for a specific experimental domain — allowing distributed design teams (e.g., for a future circular collider) to pool simulations into a shared community model. BAX has been demonstrated for accelerator control and design at SLAC, and is being applied to materials science and drug design.

Javier Duarte (UC San Diego, CMS/LHC) explains the LHC triggering challenge: 40 million proton-proton collisions per second require a first-level hardware trigger running on FPGA farms that must reject 99.8% of events within ~1 microsecond. His group replaced simple threshold algorithms (count muons, threshold total energy) with multivariate neural networks analyzing full event topology — including b-tagging for Higgs boson identification. Beyond efficiency gains for known signals, this enabled unsupervised anomaly detection via autoencoders running at the trigger level, now deployed in CMS and ATLAS, providing years of data collected under an anomaly-sensitive trigger for potential new-physics discovery. The HLS4ML compiler framework he co-developed to synthesize neural networks for FPGAs has found applications in neuroscience, materials science, and qubit readout beyond particle physics.

Chang-Kee Jung (UC Riverside) works on near-sensor and in-memory computing for liquid argon time projection chamber (TPC) dark matter detectors. The core challenge is a data bandwidth mismatch: photon rates of ~10^10 per second far exceed what LVDS cables can carry, necessitating intelligent compression and filtering at micrometer-to-millimeter distances from the sensor. His approach involves early digitization of single photon avalanche diode (SPAD) signals, fine-grained local control, and dataflow-architecture ASICs operating within sub-100 mW power budgets — essential because any thermal increase risks boiling the liquid argon. The panel converges on co-design — jointly optimizing algorithm, hardware, and detector architecture — as the key paradigm for next-generation facilities, and closes with a candid discussion of the risk that over-reliance on AI tools may atrophy students' deep physical intuition.

Panel on AI for simulating and analyzing scientific data at the 2026 Science and AI Summit, UC Riverside, June 30, 2026, moderated by Steve Choy (UCR), with Kimmy Wu (Caltech), Simeon Bird (UCR), Jianming Bian (UC Irvine), and Scott Clark (Tallaria Scientific).

Summary

synthesis panel-sim-summary

This panel, moderated by experimental cosmologist Steve Choy (UCR), brought together researchers from observational cosmology, theoretical cosmology, particle physics, and industry to discuss how AI is transforming the simulation and analysis of scientific data. Kimmy Wu (Caltech) described how AI enables field-level inference in cosmology — bypassing the traditional intermediate step of computing summary statistics from telescope maps and instead inferring cosmological parameters directly from pixel-level data. This unlocks access to information that was analytically intractable before and allows exploration of extended dark energy and dark matter models that were previously computationally impossible to test. Simeon Bird (UCR) argued that designing simulations and designing AI models will converge: AI will be embedded directly into simulations to replace phenomenological sub-grid physics prescriptions, with black hole accretion physics (connecting Schwarzschild-radius scales to hundred-megaparsec scales) identified as the hardest outstanding problem.

Jianming Bian (UC Irvine) discussed progress toward foundation models for neutrino experiments, including use of the DINO self-supervised model and vision-language models that allow human input to guide reconstruction across detectors with different geometries and resolutions. He identified three major obstacles: data availability and inter-collaboration secrecy in high-energy physics, the challenge of unifying heterogeneous data formats and tokenization schemes, and the simulation-to-real gap — the fundamental inconsistency between AI performance on simulated data and on real detector data. He stressed the obligation to open the "black box" and demonstrate that AI methods are checkable and that uncertainties are reliable, in order to win over skeptical traditional physicists.

Scott Clark (Tallaria Scientific) contributed an industry perspective rooted in Bayesian optimization, arguing that the reliability bar for AI should be calibrated to the decision being made: high fidelity is required for final results, but surrogate models and neural operators (e.g., FNO) are sufficient for navigating the search space and deciding what to try next. He framed physical laws as "unit tests" that AI must not break, analogous to continuous integration in software, and warned that objective function specification — knowing precisely what you are optimizing — is the hardest and most consequential part of any AI-driven scientific workflow. The panel converged on the view that AI's greatest current impact is accelerating inference and emulation, while the deeper challenge of genuine knowledge discovery requires careful use of physical symmetries, symmetry-informed compression, and interpretable summary statistics that can be validated against neural network outputs.

Summary

synthesis panel-theory-summary

This panel — moderated by Sean Westerdale (UC Riverside) and featuring Anima Anandkumar (Caltech/NVIDIA), Alex Meiburg (Perimeter Institute/Waterloo IQC), Chunki Jung (Stony Brook), Jacob Bortnik (UCLA), and Junglu Lee (USC) — addressed the highest-level question in AI-assisted science: can AI help formulate theories, not just simulate or classify known phenomena? The discussion surfaced a core tension between AI as a computational shortcut (surrogate models, surrogate fitting) and AI as a genuine theory-discovery engine. Junglu Lee described how in hard problems like high-temperature superconductivity, the bottleneck is not compute but the absence of correct theoretical formalisms — hundreds of hypotheses exist, none can convince the field, and AI may eventually help explore and converge that space. Meiburg cautioned that physics problems are often deeply underspecified: unlike a graph-existence proof, a question like "understand this system better" has no single conclusive answer, and the physicist may not even know what theorems they want proved. He used the historical example of gases — pre-ideal-gas-law scientists could not have articulated what equations they needed — to illustrate why prompting an AI to "find a model" may be unanswerable without prior theoretical scaffolding.

Anima Anandkumar defended the epistemic value of learned representations even absent symbolic interpretability. neural operators, learning mappings between function spaces, can encode physical principles in their layer structure without reducing to equations humans recognize. She argued that generalization far beyond training distribution — especially when constrained by physics losses — is a meaningful and measurable proxy for having "learned" underlying principles, and that insisting on symbolic regression as the only valid output forecloses genuinely useful AI-discovered structure. Jacob Bortnik added that classical ML is already productive for building hypotheses from multi-instrument data (nonlinearities, cross-instrument correlations), while LLMs can sharpen and accelerate that hypothesis-formation process, though instrument-specific data wrangling and characterization remain irreducibly human tasks. Chunki Jung highlighted that the T2K-NOvA joint analysis took ten years not because of statistical or algorithmic difficulty but because of inter-collaboration politics, negotiating common analysis frameworks, and the need for step-by-step human-verified bias-free results — domains where AI has limited near-term leverage.

The panel's most pointed exchange concerned equitable access. Chunki Jung, who advises students in Madagascar earning $30/month, framed AI compute as analogous to the introduction of computers — revolutionary, but widening the gap between wealthy and developing institutions. He called for national laboratories and international bodies (Fermilab, CERN, PNNL) to provide frontier AI models and compute as institutional subscriptions available to researchers worldwide, and proposed publicly funded AI compute centers modeled on large accelerators and telescope facilities. Anima Anandkumar emphasized that public investment in computing — not just token budgets — is the central lever, and that HPC supercomputers must evolve to serve the broader AI research community. Meiburg raised a distinct but related concern: US export controls on Claude Fable effectively bar non-US nationals from accessing frontier models regardless of funding, creating a passport-based intelligence hierarchy inside international research departments. He noted the practical futility of such controls (faculty will share access) while flagging that further restrictions — prompted by API-access theft incidents — could make the situation worse. The panel converged on open-weight models and local deployment as partial mitigations, though Chunki Jung noted that even open-weight access requires local infrastructure or cloud payment, leaving the resource problem fundamentally unsolved.

Summary

synthesis gukov-sparse-summary

Sergei Gukov, director of the Merkin Center and John D. MacArthur professor at Caltech, frames mathematical discovery as the prototypical sparse reward, long-horizon problem. He observes that the same structural difficulty — very few successful signals spread over an astronomically large search space — recurs across fusion reactor design, airplane certification, robotics, and coding agents. The key insight is that reinforcement learning, while the natural framework for such problems, fails badly when rewards are so sparse that the agent never encounters positive feedback during training. His lab at Caltech focuses exclusively on designing new algorithms and architectures to unlock this class of problems, with mathematics serving as a clean testbed because the data is synthetic and the feedback loop, though slow, is unambiguous.

Gukov uses the decade-long struggle to play Montezuma's Revenge as a case study. When DeepMind's DQN algorithm achieved strong performance across most Atari games in 2013 it scored exactly zero on Montezuma's Revenge, an exploration game requiring a long sequence of purposeful steps before any reward is obtained. Despite concerted community effort — DQN variants, C-51 distributional RL, Rainbow, Impala — progress was minimal until Go-Explore finally exceeded expert human performance. The Go-Explore authors succeeded by diagnosing two failure modes in prior systems: detachment (the agent fails to return to promising states it previously found) and derailment (the agent loses track of its progress during long rollouts). This diagnostic methodology — trying many approaches, then systematically analyzing what fails — mirrors exactly how Gukov's lab operates on mathematical problems, sometimes requiring fifty to a hundred distinct algorithm attempts before finding one that works.

Two concrete mathematical problems illustrate the approach. The algebraic analog of the Hirsch conjecture (a problem in commutative algebra about monomial ideals satisfying two simultaneously hard conditions) was eventually solved and published at ICML after extensive algorithmic experimentation. Progress on Andrews's conjecture from group theory — which asks whether every balanced presentation of the trivial group can be connected to the trivial presentation via a small set of moves — revealed a subtler difficulty: a bimodal data distribution in which synthetically generated examples cluster into an "easy pile" and a "hard pile" with nothing in between. Training on easy examples teaches the model nothing useful about the hard cases; filling the "missing middle" was the key algorithmic insight.

Gukov closes by arguing that the problems blocking AI in mathematics — out-of-distribution generalization, bimodal data, exponential search spaces — are the same problems blocking AI in extreme weather prediction, financial crash modeling, and any domain where the most important events are rare. Mathematics is attractive as a research vehicle precisely because experiments are cheap: no proprietary data, no physical machinery, just synthetic generation and clean reward signals. He paraphrases Vladimir Arnold's remark that "mathematics is a part of physics where experiments are cheap" by proposing that mathematics can be viewed as a part of AI research where data and machinery are cheap, making it an ideal laboratory for developing the algorithms that will eventually generalize.

Summary

synthesis tao-infra-summary

Terence Tao opened by observing that mathematics is one of the most tradition-bound disciplines in academia — mathematicians still favor blackboards, work in small groups, and produce knowledge through a slow handcrafted process that has changed little in centuries. While AI is now capable of generating vast numbers of proposed solutions to mathematical problems and can sometimes verify them automatically, the rest of the mathematical life cycle — peer review, digestion, communication, and incorporation into textbooks — has not scaled to match. This mismatch is creating what Tao calls "proof indigestion": traffic jams in which floods of AI-generated papers overwhelm journals and human referees, and solution repositories accumulate faster than anyone can verify them.

To illustrate the structural problem, Tao offered an automobile analogy. When cars first appeared in the 19th century, they had to share narrow cobblestone streets built for pedestrians and horses — the result was chaos. The solution was not to improve the cars but to redesign the infrastructure: dedicated roads, pedestrian walkways, traffic rules, and hybrid zones. Tao argues mathematics is at that same inflection point. Traditional journals and conferences remain valuable but cannot absorb unrestricted AI use; new infrastructure is needed that accommodates AI contributions safely while preserving space for human mathematical work.

The SAIR Foundation is developing that infrastructure through a series of crowdsourced competitions. The first, the "distillation challenge" launched on Pi Day 2026, builds on an earlier project that generated 22 million true/false questions in equational algebra. Modern frontier models can answer nearly 99% of these questions individually, but that is expensive and reveals nothing about the underlying structure. The challenge asks contestants to write a one-page "cheat sheet" — readable by both humans and cheap open-source models — that distills the essential rules governing all 22 million answers. Early results show that the best cheat sheets lift weak-model accuracy from ~51% to ~80%, and a second phase pushing toward full proofs rather than true/false answers is underway.

The second featured competition, developed in partnership with the L-functions and Modular Forms Database (LMFDB), targets the inverse Galois problem. Contestants search for polynomials with specific Galois groups, framed as an "Easter egg hunt" across 160,000 possible group types. Verification is automatic and nearly instantaneous, making the task well-suited to large-scale crowdsourcing and AI assistance. The competitive phase rewards teams who find rare group types that others have not yet found; a cooperative phase will follow in which all polynomials are shared openly. The competition is producing the first large-scale empirical dataset on the distribution of Galois groups — something traditional mathematics, not being an experimental science, has lacked. Tao noted that these competitions represent a model for how AI capabilities can be directed toward tasks that are orthogonal to, rather than competitive with, traditional mathematical work.

synthesis ucr-welcome-summary

UCR Chancellor Hoo opened the 2026 Science and AI Summit by welcoming participants and acknowledging co-organizers: the Center for Experimental Cosmology and Instrumentation at UC Riverside and the Global Foundation for Science and AI Research (SAIR Foundation). He highlighted UCR's recently founded RACE Institute (Riverside Artificial Intelligence Research and Education), which unites over 85 interdisciplinary faculty across all seven colleges to advance machine learning and develop ethical, transparent AI solutions. The chancellor emphasized that UCR, as the only R1 research university in inland Southern California, occupies a distinctive position where technology, agricultural legacy, climate pressures, and global logistics intersect.

A central theme of the chancellor's remarks was the relationship between fundamental and applied research. Using a "master key" metaphor, he argued that translational research produces keys that open particular doors, while fundamental, curiosity-driven science produces a master key capable of opening many doors — including doors not yet known to exist. He stressed that foundational inquiry into physics, mathematics, and the nature of intelligence is the necessary bedrock for building robust next-generation AI systems.

Chuck Ng, co-founder of SAIR Foundation, framed the summit around SIR's founding conviction: that science and AI must be brought into the same room with equal seriousness to work on the same problems. He outlined three programmatic tracks for the day — AI's role in designing and operating scientific detectors, AI for simulating and analyzing scientific data at previously impossible scales, and AI as a tool for theory-building that changes how science thinks, not merely computes. He also noted Southern California and the Inland Empire as an underappreciated concentration of scientific talent and emerging technology.

Nobel laureate Barry Barish, co-founder of SAIR Foundation and part-time faculty at UC Riverside since 2018, described the institutional gap he identified in the university system: universities hire physicists to analyze data and publish results, but systematically underinvest in instrumentation expertise and laboratory technology development. The fields he highlighted — dark matter searches, cosmic microwave background studies, next-generation gravitational wave detectors — sit between physics and astronomy departments and require precisely the kind of deep technical infrastructure that universities tend not to build. The CECI group at UCR represents a deliberate effort to close that gap, and the SAIR Foundation's mission is to integrate AI into that instrumentation and analysis work.
