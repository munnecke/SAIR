## Summary

The panel brought together academic leaders and investors to assess the Southern California innovation landscape, with broad agreement that the region is performing well but operating well below its potential — one panelist estimated three to ten times under-capacity relative to available talent, research output, and industrial diversity. Participants drew a consistent contrast with the Bay Area model: Southern California founders tend to build cash-flow businesses that solve real problems with willing payers, often retaining far greater ownership than Bay Area counterparts who repeatedly dilute through successive financing rounds. This capital-efficient culture was framed as a distinctive strength, not a limitation.

UC Riverside's identity was highlighted as shaped directly by its Inland Empire geography. The campus originated in 1907 as a citrus experimental station, and agriculture remains a core strength. Panelists used Chancellor Hoo's concept of "comparative agriculture" to describe UCR's position as a living laboratory for air quality, water quality, and crop optimization — domains where AI-assisted research could generate both scientific advances and direct economic benefit for the region. The physics and astronomy chair stressed that transformative technology ultimately rests on basic science investment, arguing for world-class laboratory infrastructure as the single highest-leverage accelerant for the region.

The UCLA computer science perspective emphasized the urgency of technology transfer pathways that do not require faculty to leave academia. A proposed AI Innovation Center, anchored at UCLA and intended to extend across the UC system including UC Riverside, was discussed as a vehicle for enabling faculty and students to commercialize ideas without relocating to the Bay Area. Panelists agreed that the missing ingredient is not talent or research — both are abundant — but rather structured mechanisms that bring capital, clinical or industrial problem-owners, and technical experts to the same table around specific, defined problems rather than general intellectual exploration.

The investor on the panel identified the most under-appreciated opportunity as the inflow of Silicon Valley growth capital into Southern California, which could catalyze the compounding flywheel seen in mature ecosystems: exits funding new founders, first employees becoming founders, and successive generations building locally. The region's sectoral breadth — entertainment, aerospace, defense, space exploration, wireless communications, semiconductors, healthcare, life sciences, energy, logistics, and agriculture — was cited as both an asset and an underutilized one, with the panel closing on the message that researchers and students in the region are agents of change in a moment where the only binding constraints are the laws of physics.
