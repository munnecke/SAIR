## Summary

[[Alex Mberg]] argues that AI systems capable of producing long, superficially convincing mathematical proofs pose a serious epistemological risk: without [[formal verification]], we risk a future flooded with plausible-but-wrong papers that pass LM-judge smell tests but contain subtle errors. The core problem is that when you set AIs to loop until a proof convinces another AI judge, you are selecting for the most convincing-sounding wrong answer, not for correctness. The [[Lean theorem prover]] offers a structural solution — if a proof checks out in [[Lean theorem prover|Lean]], it is correct regardless of how confusing the intermediate steps are — but this only works if the theorem statement itself is correctly formulated.

[[Alex Mberg|Mberg]] illustrates the theorem-statement auditing problem with an Alice-and-Bob demo: he asked an AI to prove that no graph has an odd number of odd-degree vertices, and separately asked it to prove that such a graph does exist. Both times the AI produced formally verified [[Lean theorem prover|Lean]] code. The resolution is that the AI silently smuggled in opposite finiteness assumptions — one proof assumed a finite vertex set (handshaking lemma), the other an infinite one (the integer number line). [[Lean theorem prover|Lean]] accepted both because both are mathematically valid under their respective hidden hypotheses. The lesson is that theorem statements, not just proofs, require careful human auditing, and unstated side conditions are trivially exploitable by LMs.

Physics presents special difficulties compared to pure mathematics. Entire subfields operate within a single approximation (e.g., condensed matter routinely assumes nuclei are stationary — the Born-Oppenheimer approximation — without stating it), and standard physics textbooks do not make these side conditions explicit. Formalizing even something as elementary as Maxwell's equations requires choosing among point charges, surface charges, curved spacetime, nonlinear media, and perfect conductors, producing a definition so general it becomes unusable. The "angle of repose" example illustrates how a trivially obvious physical phenomenon requires enormous mathematical scaffolding to formalize rigorously.

[[Alex Mberg|Mberg]]'s proposed path forward is a physics analogue of [[MathLib]]: a community-consensus library (FizzLib) that standardizes definitions the way Bourbaki standardized analysis and topology. He advocates using moonshot targets — such as formalizing the MIP*=RE result in [[quantum computing|quantum]] information theory — to force the community to build out broadly reusable libraries rather than each group rolling their own isolated definitions. A completed formalization of MIP*=RE would necessarily yield a substantial quantum information library that others could build on, just as [[Lean theorem prover|Lean]]'s formalization of Fermat's Last Theorem forced a large body of reusable number theory into [[MathLib]].
