## Summary

This panel — moderated by Sean Westerdale (UC Riverside) and featuring Anima Anandkumar (Caltech/NVIDIA), Alex Mberg (Perimeter Institute/Waterloo IQC), Chunki Jung (Stony Brook), Jacob Bortnik (UCLA), and Junglu Lee (USC) — addressed the highest-level question in AI-assisted science: can AI help formulate theories, not just simulate or classify known phenomena? The discussion surfaced a core tension between AI as a computational shortcut (surrogate models, surrogate fitting) and AI as a genuine theory-discovery engine. Junglu Lee described how in hard problems like high-temperature superconductivity, the bottleneck is not compute but the absence of correct theoretical formalisms — hundreds of hypotheses exist, none can convince the field, and AI may eventually help explore and converge that space. Alex Mberg cautioned that physics problems are often deeply underspecified: unlike a graph-existence proof, a question like "understand this system better" has no single conclusive answer, and the physicist may not even know what theorems they want proved. He used the historical example of gases — pre-ideal-gas-law scientists could not have articulated what equations they needed — to illustrate why prompting an AI to "find a model" may be unanswerable without prior theoretical scaffolding.

Anima Anandkumar defended the epistemic value of learned representations even absent symbolic interpretability. Neural operators, learning mappings between function spaces, can encode physical principles in their layer structure without reducing to equations humans recognize. She argued that generalization far beyond training distribution — especially when constrained by physics losses — is a meaningful and measurable proxy for having "learned" underlying principles, and that insisting on symbolic regression as the only valid output forecloses genuinely useful AI-discovered structure. Jacob Bortnik added that classical ML is already productive for building hypotheses from multi-instrument data (nonlinearities, cross-instrument correlations), while LLMs can sharpen and accelerate that hypothesis-formation process, though instrument-specific data wrangling and characterization remain irreducibly human tasks. Chunki Jung highlighted that the T2K-NOvA joint analysis took ten years not because of statistical or algorithmic difficulty but because of inter-collaboration politics, negotiating common analysis frameworks, and the need for step-by-step human-verified bias-free results — domains where AI has limited near-term leverage.

The panel's most pointed exchange concerned equitable access. Chunki Jung, who advises students in Madagascar earning $30/month, framed AI compute as analogous to the introduction of computers — revolutionary, but widening the gap between wealthy and developing institutions. He called for national laboratories and international bodies (Fermilab, CERN, PNNL) to provide frontier AI models and compute as institutional subscriptions available to researchers worldwide, and proposed publicly funded AI compute centers modeled on large accelerators and telescope facilities. Anima Anandkumar emphasized that public investment in computing — not just token budgets — is the central lever, and that HPC supercomputers must evolve to serve the broader AI research community. Alex Mberg raised a distinct but related concern: US export controls on Claude Fable effectively bar non-US nationals from accessing frontier models regardless of funding, creating a passport-based intelligence hierarchy inside international research departments. He noted the practical futility of such controls (faculty will share access) while flagging that further restrictions — prompted by API-access theft incidents — could make the situation worse. The panel converged on open-weight models and local deployment as partial mitigations, though Chunki Jung noted that even open-weight access requires local infrastructure or cloud payment, leaving the resource problem fundamentally unsolved.
