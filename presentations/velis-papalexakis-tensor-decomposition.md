## Summary

Evangelos ([[Velis Papalexakis]]) presents a framework for knowledge discovery from data built around [[tensor decomposition]] — an approach he has developed over 15+ years that bridges classical linear-algebraic methods with modern AI. He defines knowledge discovery as the process of extracting insights from complex, high-dimensional data that are novel, interesting, and scarce enough to be actionable by a domain expert (scientist or experimentalist). These insights fall into three categories: descriptive (characterizing latent structure), predictive (anticipating unmeasured outcomes), and prescriptive (recommending which experiment or design to test next). A central challenge in scientific settings is the absence of supervision — there are no clear labels for what is "interesting" — and the consequent need for explainability, physics-informed modeling, and active engagement of the domain expert in the loop.

The mathematical core of the talk is [[tensor decomposition|tensor factorization]] and its key property of uniqueness. Whereas matrix factorization recovers latent components only up to an arbitrary rotation of subspaces — leaving the solution underdetermined — higher-order tensor decompositions can recover the true underlying components up to permutation and scaling, under mild conditions. This uniqueness is essential for making scientifically interpretable claims: without it, any identified "pattern" might simply be one of infinitely many equivalent rotations of noise. [[Velis Papalexakis|Papalexakis]] illustrates this with a Netflix-style example (users × movies × time) before moving to scientific applications.

Two classical (pre-AI) scientific applications anchor the talk. The first is [[LIGO]] glitch identification: LIGO's extreme sensitivity causes it to record non-transient noise artifacts (glitches) that can mask or mimic [[gravitational waves|gravitational-wave]] signals. By constructing a tensor of (channel × glitch event × frequency) across tens of thousands of auxiliary sensor channels, [[tensor decomposition|tensor factorization]] identifies small sets of channels whose coincident activation explains specific glitch families — providing testable hypotheses about the physical mechanism producing each glitch class. The second application is materials design via tensor completion: when only a sparse subset of a combinatorial design space has been synthesized, tensor completion (which exploits the learned latent space to impute missing entries) can generalize to the full parameter space from cheap-region training data and identify the best candidate material for a target property, while the recovered tensor factors also surface interpretable correlations between design parameters that generate new hypotheses for the lab.

The final section shows how LLMs augment this classical pipeline. Using in-context learning (providing example (input, label) pairs directly in the prompt without weight updates), an LLM can automatically annotate behavioral data — specifically, mouse behavior in a PTSD/fear-generalization neuroscience experiment — at accuracy approaching a trained domain expert and well above conventional ML baselines. A vision-language model (VLM) is then used to score each discovered tensor component on "worthiness of discovery," drawing on behavioral neuroscience literature injected as context, and to generate written descriptions of why a component merits attention. The resulting descriptions closely matched those written by the graduate student experimentalist on held-out data, demonstrating a practical pipeline: [[tensor decomposition]] finds latent patterns; LLM/VLM in-context learning ranks and narrates them; and the experimentalist focuses on the highest-value hypotheses.
